Meal Matchup


Meal Matchup is a web and mobile application that facilitates the donation of leftover food from University of Washington dining halls to local non-profits in the Seattle area.

This project is backed by UWâ€™s award winning $17,000 from Campus sustainability fund and will be implemented in UW dining halls in Fall 2019.

Team size: 4

Overview



The Problem

40 percent of all U.S food goes uneaten yearly, resulting in 133 billion pounds wasted. Food wastage is responsible for many social evils such as climatic change and hunger. How might we reduce food wastage at the University Of Washington (UW), by distributing the leftover food that is good for consumption?

Design Question

How do we design a sustainable solution to reduce food wastage while feeding people who are food insecure?

UX Research

I started by interviewing student workers at the Housing and Food services (HFS) at the University of Washington. Based on the interview findings, we came up with the affinity diagram.


Research Findings

Scattered Information

  • There is no centralized place where all the stakeholders can see the information. Information is stored in a mixture of paper and digital form, making it hard to track.

Ineffecient Communication

  • Dining halls currently rely on phone calls and email to contact non-profits and check their availability. This process is time consuming as they have to manually go through contact list.

Accountability

  • Due to lack of resources, non-profits sometimes have to cancel the scheduled pick-ups at the last minute. Dining halls want to minimize missed donations. Both dining halls and non-profits have limited resources when it comes to transportation and employees. They need additional support for delivery and distribution.

Our Approach

  • As non-profit organizations, food shelters are always short of labor and transportation resources, making them hard to travel to UW to pick up food. To solve this, we recommended student volunteers, who are willing to participate in food delivery. The ideal situation will connect all stakeholders (dining hall managers, food shelter workers, student volunteers) and redirect food away from compost to local shelter in need.
  • This recommendation led us to change our initial design, which did not involve student volunteers.

Process Flow

Design Iterations

Calendar was the most critical feature in the product as it displays information about upcoming deliveries. The calendar originally had a static side bar with information about the day that would update depending on the day which was clicked. This turned out to be confusing to the participants who failed to notice that the side bar populated with new information. Therefore, we iterated on the calendar and decided to pop-up the information on the selected date, which received positive feedback.

Our solution

We came up with a mobile app that notifies the student volunteer about the pickup. When the student volunteer taps on the notification, he/she is taken to the screen where he/she is informed about the location to pickup the food donation and the recipient address. When the user taps on the location icon, the app opens the local map and the student volunteers can access the location on the map.
